Design Manifesto — April 2026

The Anchor
House

18136 Riviera Place SW · Normandy Park, WA 98166

The Truth About This House

This house has extraordinary bones. A double-height glass curtain wall framing Puget Sound sunsets. Three livable levels carved into a hillside. Vertical volume that rivals anything you’d find in a Dwell feature. At dusk, it glows from within like a lantern in the forest.

It also has a confused soul.

The 1991 builder took genuinely ambitious architectural moves and dressed them in the decorative language of the era: lattice grid railings, stepped “wedding cake” drywall soffits that soften every corner into mush, a stacked ledgestone accent wall that reads “hotel lobby,” and a boxy traditional fireplace mantel sitting right at the heart of the home’s most important spatial moment. Subsequent owners layered on gray faux-wood vinyl flooring, a glossy dark kitchen with pure flipper energy, and chrome hardware with no point of view.

(Worth noting: the glass block elements—particularly the curved wall in the laundry—are structurally interesting and dated enough to be cool. We’re keeping those. They become conversation pieces, not problems, once the surrounding materials are elevated.)

We are not remodeling this house. We are performing an architectural excavation.

We’re removing every layer of confused stylistic decision to expose the spatial logic that was always there—and then inserting a precise, limited vocabulary of honest materials that give the house a coherent identity for the first time in its life.

The double-height glass wall — the soul of the house, currently undermined by its surroundings

The Aesthetic

Modern warmth in the wild. That’s the shorthand. Architecturally daring but grounded—not cold, not slick, not performing minimalism. Natural materials that age honestly. Bold but intentional color. Interiors filled with objects that are handmade, sculptural, or genuinely interesting. A home that belongs to its landscape while expressing a distinct creative personality.

Sea Ranch = The Macro

Embrace the volume. Honor the view. Don’t fight the drama of the glass wall. This house is perched above the Sound and wrapped in PNW forest—the architecture should amplify that, not compete with it. At twilight, the house should glow from inside while the trees press against it from outside. That tension is the point.

Japandi = The Micro

Every line is intentional. Every transition is considered. Nothing is arbitrary. Scandinavian restraint with warmth—clean but not sterile, calm but not empty. This energy governs the details: how a floor meets a wall, how a slat pattern terminates, how a doorway frames what’s beyond it.

Schoolhouse Electric = The Release Valve

The oxblood powder room. The rust velvet upholstery. The industrial cylindrical hood. The orange faucet that makes someone stop and grin. Without this energy, the restraint calcifies into preciousness. This house needs moments that are weird, playful, and unapologetically bold. Your house needs more weird stuff—we believe that.

The Material Language

One wood. One tone. One metal. One geometry. That’s the discipline. Everything flows from four material commitments.

The Vertical Language: “Ship’s Bones”

Vertical white oak slats are the repeating architectural motif that ties the house together—dining room, master bath, and eventually theater. They’re not decoration. They’re the structural rhythm of the home, the way a ship’s ribs give a hull its shape.

The Rule

No bronze. No brass. No chrome. No exceptions. All fixtures, switches, pulls, and handles are dark, matte, and knurled—industrial precision without coldness.

The Latent Material: Corten Steel

Not in the current scope, but deeply present in our design instinct. Weathered steel—a material that ages rather than degrades—is the missing thread between the blackened hardware and the warm wood. We’re open to it showing up: an exterior accent, a custom hood detail, a shelving bracket. It belongs in this house’s future.

The Interventions

Phase 1 · Core Identity

1. The Totem

The living/dining pivot. The center of gravity. The thing that gives this house its name.

Current: boxy mantel, drywall columns, no spatial purpose
Concept render — directional, not prescriptive

The existing fireplace is a boxy, mantel-framed traditional surround with drywall columns—the single worst offender in the house. It sits at the most important spatial moment and contributes nothing.

The replacement: A floor-to-ceiling sculptural cylinder in hand-troweled microcement, continuous and uninterrupted, piercing through the loft line to the vaulted ceiling above. Ribbed or fluted to catch light. Warm “bone” tone. Fire visible from both living and dining sides.

2. The Foundation

Select grade white oak throughout the entire main floor. Fix the subfloor dip near the current fireplace. And the unglamorous move that pays the biggest dividends: remove the “wedding cake” drywall steps throughout the house. Corners become square. Lines become clean. Deceptively simple work with outsized visual impact.

3. The Workshop (Kitchen)

Current kitchen — functional but lacks material intention

The strategy is “The IKEA Hack.” Standard SEKTION cabinet boxes fitted with custom plywood fronts in a Kerf style. Maximum visual impact through intelligent constraint.

Layout: Refrigerator relocates to the back wall to unblock visual flow. A cylindrical “tube” hood suspends from the ceiling as a sculptural industrial element, not an appliance.

Finishes: Two-tone color strategy. Natural birch uppers (warm, unpainted) vs. matte Schoolhouse Green or Slate lowers. Countertops in white Richlite—soft, matte, warm. Not stone. Not quartz. Paper.

4. The Refractor (Dining Wall)

Current: stacked ledgestone accent wall with oversized mirror

The ledgestone reads as Ramada Inn. The mirror actually does useful work—but the execution is wrong. The replacement: vertical white oak slats over smoked mirror. The slats screen; the mirror reads as depth, not reflection. A linear suspension fixture in blackened steel hangs low to create intimacy.

5. The Roundabout

A social pause point. The existing built-in seating gets re-clad in Kerf-style plywood. Upholstery in saturated wool or velvet—Rust or Mustard. Clear volume overhead. A Vestaboard on the rear wall because that’s exactly the kind of weird, narrative object this house wants.

Phase 2 · The Retreat

6. The Powder Room

This room does not need to be calm. It needs to hit. Deep, unexpected saturated color on the walls—Oxblood, Teal, or Charcoal. Colorful but timeless floor tile. An industrial wall-hung sink. This is a Schoolhouse Electric moment turned up to eleven. The kind of room where someone closes the door, looks around, and says “holy shit.”

7. The Nordic Spa (Master Suite)

Current master bath — the space exists, the vision doesn’t

The transition: A walk-through closet vestibule that builds a psychological threshold—a pause between Sleep and Wake.

The bathroom: Radiant heated floors in stone-look porcelain or terrazzo. Furniture-style vanity in warm wood with pill-shaped mirrors. The tiny shower and cavernous toilet room merge into a unified spa: large curbless shower, toilet in a niche, and a soaking tub clad in vertical white oak slats. The tub becomes architecture, not a fixture.

Phase 3 · The Shell

Exterior + Lower Level + Outdoor Living

Not scoped yet, but named so it’s on the map. The lower-level theater and kitchenette want the same material language brought downstairs. The exterior hasn’t been touched by this vision yet. And the backyard is begging for intervention: outdoor living, possibly a sauna, replacing the synthetic turf with something honest.

We see this as a multi-year design partnership, not a one-and-done—a sustained collaboration where each phase extends the vocabulary established in the first.

Budget + Phasing

Total budget: $225,000–$250,000 (design + construction, all phases).

This scope is ambitious relative to budget. We know that. The manifesto is the full vision; the phasing is the reality. We’re not precious about doing it all at once. We are precious about doing each phase right.

Phase 1
Core Identity
$140,000 – $160,000

The Totem, white oak floors, wedding cake removal, kitchen renovation, and the Refractor dining wall. Delivers ~80% of the visual transformation and establishes the material language completely.

Phase 2
The Retreat
$60,000 – $80,000

Master bathroom Nordic Spa conversion, Roundabout re-cladding, powder room.

We’ve made smart budget decisions: the IKEA Hack kitchen saves 40–60% vs. full custom. Richlite is significantly cheaper than natural stone. Microcement over armature is dramatically less than poured concrete. The subtractive work—demolishing the wedding cake steps, pulling the ledgestone—is labor, not expensive material.

These interventions are specific but not prescriptive—they establish clear intention while leaving generous room for architectural refinement, surprising ideas, and the kind of collaboration that makes a project better than either party could alone.
